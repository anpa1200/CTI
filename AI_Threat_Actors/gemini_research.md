The Evolution of AI Usage by Threat Actors: A Comprehensive Analytical ReportExecutive SummaryThe integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) into offensive cyber operations represents one of the most profound paradigm shifts in the history of the global threat landscape. For decades, cybercriminals and state-sponsored advanced persistent threats (APTs) relied on static automation, pre-compiled tooling, and human-intensive reconnaissance to execute campaigns. However, the rapid maturation of large language models (LLMs), generative adversarial networks (GANs), and increasingly, agentic AI frameworks, has fundamentally altered both the economics and the mechanics of cyberattacks. This report delivers an exhaustive, evidence-based analysis of how threat actors have weaponized AI over time, tracing the historical trajectory from early statistical evasion techniques deployed against rudimentary spam filters to contemporary, runtime-generated malware and multimodal social engineering heists.Extensive telemetry and threat intelligence analysis reveals that the immediate, verifiable impact of AI lies predominantly in the acceleration, scale, and linguistic perfection of existing tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), rather than the spontaneous generation of entirely novel attack vectors. Generative AI has effectively democratized advanced social engineering, dramatically lowering the barrier to entry for financially motivated cybercriminals. The statistical outcomes are stark: AI-generated polymorphic phishing campaigns currently achieve a 54% click-through rate, vastly outperforming the 12% success rate of traditional, human-crafted lures. Simultaneously, the deployment of high-fidelity deepfakes has bypassed traditional biometric and visual identity verification protocols, resulting in massive financial losses, such as the widely documented $25 million corporate heist executed against the engineering firm Arup in 2024.Beyond fraud and social engineering, elite state-nexus actors have crossed a critical technical threshold. Threat intelligence from late 2025 and early 2026 confirms that APTs are transitioning from using AI strictly as an offline reconnaissance and coding assistant to embedding AI directly into malware execution chains. Malware families such as PROMPTSTEAL and PROMPTFLUX now utilize live API queries to external LLMs during runtime to generate dynamic, environment-specific commands and achieve metamorphic code obfuscation, rendering static signature-based detection largely obsolete.Nevertheless, the cybersecurity ecosystem remains heavily clouded by vendor-driven hype and misattribution. Widespread claims of "fully autonomous" hacking campaigns orchestrated by rogue, independent AI systems remain largely unverified, heavily caveated, or representative of academic proof-of-concepts rather than pervasive real-world threats. Moving forward, the most critical vulnerability for modern enterprises is not the specter of an omnipotent AI hacker, but rather the fragility of the AI supply chain itself. As organizations rapidly adopt agentic AI frameworks, attackers are aggressively pivoting to compromise the underlying dependencies, open-source proxy libraries, and API credentials that power these systems, seeking to weaponize the enterprise's own AI infrastructure against it.1. Operators with minimal technical expertise can now execute highly targeted, multi-lingual Business Email Compromise (BEC) and synthetic media fraud campaigns using illicit, underground LLMs.Medium Confidence: Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) are actively transitioning from utilizing LLMs as offline productivity tools to embedding API-driven AI calls directly into malware payloads to achieve dynamic runtime obfuscation, metamorphic evasion, and environment-tailored execution.High Confidence: Pervasive industry claims of "fully autonomous" or "agentic" cyberattacks occurring in the wild are currently overstated. While agentic AI represents a severe, escalating risk vector, present-day incidents touted as autonomous typically require substantial human scaffolding, rely on standard open-source tools, or reflect laboratory-bound research environments.Medium Confidence: Over the immediate 12 to 36-month horizon, supply chain attacks targeting core AI infrastructure—such as the compromise of open-source AI proxy dependencies, model poisoning, and credential theft via AI middleware—will eclipse the direct adversarial misuse of AI in terms of systemic, cascading enterprise risk.2. Analysis of Common TTPs Where AI is UsedTo accurately assess the evolution of AI in cybercrime, the technology must be mapped against the MITRE ATT&CK framework. While fully autonomous, end-to-end execution remains elusive, AI has measurably augmented almost every phase of the cyber kill chain. This section addresses another core question: When did AI shift from fraud and social engineering support into intrusion support? The telemetry indicates this pivot occurred between late 2024 and mid-2025, driven by APT experimentation with runtime API integration and LLM-assisted vulnerability research.ReconnaissanceMechanism: Threat actors heavily leverage LLMs to rapidly summarize target corporate structures, scrape open-source intelligence (OSINT), profile key executives, and analyze massive troves of public vulnerability data.Status: Confirmed and highly prevalent.Examples: North Korean groups such as Emerald Sleet use LLMs to deeply research complex vulnerabilities (e.g., CVE-2022-30190) and identify potential exploitation paths far more efficiently than manual analysis allows. Iranian actor UNC1860 utilizes Gemini to research SQL injection techniques tailored to specific target architectures.Limitations: Reconnaissance is limited by the training data cutoff dates of the models used, though actors bypass this by using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) tied to live internet scraping.Resource DevelopmentMechanism: AI is fundamentally transforming how attackers build their infrastructure and operational personas. This includes generating high-fidelity deepfake imagery for fake personas, writing persuasive content for fake corporate websites, and standing up convincing, high-trust command-and-control (C2) web infrastructure.Status: Confirmed and standard practice.Examples: The North Korean cluster Jasper Sleet routinely uses the AI application Faceswap to fabricate identity documents and polished headshots for remote IT worker job applications. In the underground, developers created WormGPT and FraudGPT, fine-tuning older open-source models (like GPT-J) specifically on malware and phishing datasets.Initial AccessMechanism: Attackers are using AI to optimize their entry vectors. While zero-day exploitation generated purely by AI remains theoretical, AI is used to process massive amounts of network telemetry to find misconfigurations. Furthermore, sophisticated actors are targeting edge devices to route traffic.Status: Confirmed execution support.Examples: Forest Blizzard (Storm-2754) hijacked thousands of vulnerable SOHO routers globally to execute Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) attacks. While the exploit itself was traditional, the scale of traffic interception requires automated filtering to triage high-value targets from massive datasets.Social Engineering / PhishingMechanism: This is the most mature application of offensive AI. The automated generation of hyper-personalized spear-phishing campaigns allows models to scrape a target's LinkedIn, perfectly mimic the tone of corporate communications, and generate polymorphic email variants that easily bypass static email security gateways.Status: Confirmed, ubiquitous, and highly effective.Examples: AI-driven phishing currently yields approximately a 54% click-through rate, completely changing the economics of credential harvesting compared to human-crafted campaigns. AI is also used to generate multi-lingual lures, expanding the geographic reach of regional cybercriminals.Malware Development / Script GenerationMechanism: Threat actors primarily use LLMs as "co-pilots" to scaffold initial scripts, translate malware between programming languages (e.g., from Python to Rust or Go to avoid detection), and debug complex exploits. A smaller, more advanced subset uses "Just-in-Time" API calls where the malware queries an LLM dynamically.Status: Co-pilot usage is ubiquitous; runtime dynamic execution is confirmed but restricted to advanced APTs.Examples: The experimental PROMPTFLUX dropper queries the Gemini API to rewrite its own source code continuously.Limitations: LLMs frequently hallucinate or introduce syntax errors when asked to develop complex zero-day exploits from scratch. Human oversight and manual debugging remain strictly necessary for highly functional, novel exploit code.Command-and-Control (C2) SupportMechanism: AI is used to automate the deployment of C2 infrastructure and generate highly realistic, polymorphic beaconing traffic that mimics legitimate API or web browsing activity, making network defense and anomaly detection exceedingly difficult.Status: Confirmed use in infrastructure setup.Examples: North Korean actor Coral Sleet utilizes AI to rapidly design, configure, and troubleshoot tunneling infrastructure, allowing them to cycle through clean, high-trust IP addresses at scale.Credential Theft / Fraud EnablementMechanism: AI is deployed to circumvent biometric security through face swap attacks on ID verification systems. Inside compromised networks, AI tools are used to rapidly parse unstructured data dumps to locate hidden API keys and hardcoded passwords.Status: Confirmed and accelerating rapidly.Examples: The QUIETVAULT credential stealer leverages an embedded AI prompt and on-host AI CLI tools to autonomously search infected host systems for specific developer secrets, such as GitHub and NPM tokens, prior to exfiltration.Defense EvasionMechanism: The holy grail of AI malware is true metamorphic code generation. Malware utilizes AI APIs to rewrite its own source code or obfuscate payloads dynamically upon execution, altering its cryptographic hash and behavioral signature on every single deployment.Status: Confirmed in active operations, though relatively rare due to operational instability.Examples: The Russian APT28 deployment of PROMPTSTEAL, which queries an LLM to generate execution commands dynamically rather than relying on hardcoded logic that endpoint detection and response (EDR) solutions easily flag.PersistenceMechanism: Ensuring malware remains on a system across reboots. AI is used to dynamically generate varied registry keys, scheduled tasks, or startup scripts tailored to the specific OS version of the victim.Status: Confirmed in experimental and targeted attacks.Examples: The PROMPTFLUX family achieves regeneration and persistence by prompting an LLM to rewrite its own source code and continuously saving the newly obfuscated version directly into the Windows Startup folder.Privilege EscalationMechanism: Utilizing AI to map internal Active Directory structures, identify misconfigured permissions, and suggest paths to domain administrator access. In modern environments, this extends to abusing the privileges of deployed AI agents.Status: Confirmed via tool misuse.Examples: As enterprise "agentic" AI systems are granted read/write API access to databases, attackers are focusing on indirect prompt injections to hijack the agent's goal state, causing the AI itself to escalate privileges on behalf of the attacker.Exfiltration / MonetizationMechanism: The post-compromise deployment of AI to analyze terabytes of stolen data. Ransomware affiliates use AI to quickly identify sensitive intellectual property, financial records, and PII to prioritize for extortion, drastically reducing the time spent inside the network.Status: Confirmed and highly utilized.Examples: This AI-driven parsing has been a primary factor in driving the Mean Time to Exfiltrate (MTTE) down from weeks to a matter of minutes. AI is also used by affiliates to draft persuasive, professional extortion emails and manage multi-victim negotiations.5. Separating Reality from HypeThe cybersecurity industry is currently experiencing a severe hype cycle regarding AI threats. Vendors routinely conflate basic automation with "artificial intelligence" to capitalize on market anxiety. Distinguishing between genuine operational shifts and speculative marketing is paramount for effective enterprise risk management.The Myth of "Fully Autonomous" Hacking:
A pervasive narrative suggests that rogue AI systems can independently identify targets, write novel zero-day exploits, and execute end-to-end campaigns without human intervention. This is currently false. The much-publicized November 2025 incident—where Anthropic claimed its Claude Code AI was hijacked by Chinese hackers to execute a "large-scale cyberattack without substantial human intervention"—was heavily scrutinized and subsequently downplayed by the broader threat intelligence community. Independent researchers from Intel 471 and Palo Alto Networks noted that the AI frequently "hallucinated" credentials, wrote bug-ridden exploit code that failed to execute, and ultimately relied entirely on standard open-source penetration tools deployed and supervised by human operators. While "agentic" cyberattacks remain a focal point of research, they are primarily theoretical or confined to highly controlled, sanitized sandbox environments rather than roaming the wild internet.The Misunderstanding of "AI Ransomware":
In late 2025, alarming reports circulated about PROMPTLOCK, widely touted as the "first AI-powered ransomware" discovered in the wild. In reality, PROMPTLOCK was an academic proof-of-concept developed by researchers at NYU Tandon to simulate how a local LLM could autonomously identify files and generate encryption scripts. It was uploaded to VirusTotal for standard academic testing, where it was mistakenly identified by security vendors as an active, malicious threat. While it proves technical feasibility, actual ransomware operators continue to rely on highly optimized, mathematically proven encryption algorithms (like those used by ALPHV and LockBit) rather than trusting a slow, unpredictable, and hallucination-prone LLM to handle the critical encryption phase of an attack.What is Genuinely Changing:
The reality of AI in offensive operations is more mundane but arguably more dangerous: efficiency, scale, and language democratization. AI is effectively eliminating the traditional "attacker bottleneck." A single threat actor can now translate a complex phishing lure into 40 languages flawlessly, spin up 100 deepfake videos of a target CEO, and debug a complex Python script in minutes. The true, verified shift is the application of AI to the "left side" of the kill chain (reconnaissance, social engineering, and weaponization), allowing lower-tier criminals to operate with an operational tempo previously reserved exclusively for well-funded, state-sponsored syndicates.7. Actor Segmentation: Comparative AI UsageThe adoption curve of AI capabilities is not uniform across the threat landscape. Different categories of threat actors are integrating AI at different speeds, based entirely on their specific operational constraints, primary objectives, and resourcing.Financially Motivated Cybercrime and Fraud Actors (Highest Adoption):
Fraudsters and Business Email Compromise (BEC) actors are the most prolific and mature adopters of AI. Because their primary operational bottleneck is convincing human targets to willingly part with assets or credentials, generative text and synthetic media offer immediate, highly lucrative ROI. Tools like WormGPT and FraudGPT have allowed low-skilled actors to operate globally, completely eroding the traditional heuristic that "poor grammar equals a phishing attempt". The use of deepfakes in this sector is highly operationalized, scaling rapidly to bypass KYC identity verification and facilitate massive corporate fraud.State-Linked / APT Groups (Targeted & Advanced Adoption):
Nation-state actors are integrating AI selectively, but with the highest degree of technical sophistication. Rather than conducting mass fraud, groups like North Korea's Emerald Sleet use LLMs for deep vulnerability research, analyzing complex CVEs to find esoteric exploit paths. APT28 (Russia) has demonstrated the highest technical innovation to date by deploying PROMPTSTEAL, utilizing AI for dynamic command generation in live espionage operations. Furthermore, North Korean actors (Jasper Sleet) utilize AI extensively for identity fabrication—generating AI headshots and utilizing voice changers to seamlessly infiltrate Western tech companies as remote IT workers.Ransomware Operators (Moderate Adoption / Workflow Optimization):
Despite the aforementioned media speculation regarding "AI ransomware," the actual deployment of AI within the encryption binary is currently minimal. However, Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) affiliates extensively use AI outside the binary. Groups leverage LLMs to quickly parse exfiltrated data to find the most sensitive corporate secrets, draft highly tailored extortion emails, and manage victim negotiations. AI's primary impact in the ransomware ecosystem is reducing the "mean time to exfiltrate" (MTTE) and automating the heavy administrative burden of triple-extortion campaigns.Influence Operations and Hacktivists (High Adoption / Low Sophistication):
State-aligned influence operations (e.g., the Iranian actor STORM-0817) heavily utilize commercial LLMs to scrape social media, generate long-form divisive political articles in multiple languages, and deploy conversational bots. Similarly, politically motivated hacktivists, who generally lack the budget for custom exploit development, utilize open-source LLMs to generate mass propaganda, write simple website defacement scripts, and coordinate low-level DDoS attacks. This represents a pure volume play, utilizing AI's generative capacity to flood information ecosystems and overwhelm rudimentary defenses.8. Technical Evolution of Offensive AIThe progression of AI usage by threat actors can be charted across several distinct technological stages, reflecting the broader advancements in the commercial AI sector.Basic Automation & ML-Assisted Abuse: Early use of OCR to break CAPTCHAs, and keyword stuffing to alter Bayesian spam filter confidence scores.Generative Text for Scams: The adoption of early LLMs to fix grammar, translate lures, and automate the "Ping, Zing, Sting" romance and BEC scam workflows.Voice Cloning & Multimodal Deepfakes: The shift from text to synthetic biometric media, culminating in real-time, multi-person video spoofing to bypass corporate financial controls.LLM-Assisted Coding: The use of commercial and underground LLMs as "co-pilots" to scaffold malware, translate code between languages, and debug exploits.Runtime AI Querying (Just-in-Time Malware): The current cutting edge. Malware (like PROMPTSTEAL) containing hard-coded API keys that query external LLMs during execution to dynamically generate environment-specific commands and evade static signatures.Agentic Workflows: The near-future stage where autonomous systems are given high-level goals and iteratively chain logic to achieve them, currently seen primarily in lab environments or via compromised enterprise agents.The Next Stage (AI Supply Chain Hijacking): Attackers bypassing the need to build offensive AI entirely, instead targeting the open-source dependencies (like LiteLLM) and proxy servers that enterprises use to connect to LLMs, stealing API keys and poisoning organizational data at the source.9. Forecast and PrognosisBased on current adoption curves, underground marketplace trends, and the trajectory of commercial AI development, the threat landscape is expected to evolve along the following timelines:12-Month Forecast (2026–2027)The immediate, highest-risk vector is not an influx of AI-generated super-malware, but rather a surge in attacks against AI infrastructure. As demonstrated by the TeamPCP LiteLLM incident , threat actors will aggressively target open-source AI libraries, proxy servers, and ML repositories (such as Hugging Face). Their goal is to steal enterprise API keys, alter models, and gain downstream access to proprietary corporate data. Simultaneously, high-fidelity deepfakes will become normalized, completely bypassing legacy biometric and visual identity verification systems. Fraud instances will rise exponentially, forcing the financial sector and large enterprises to abandon video and audio verification entirely in favor of strict, cryptographic identity proofs (e.g., FIDO2).3-Year Forecast (2029)By 2029, we assess that "agentic" AI frameworks will transition from experimental enterprise productivity tools to viable, packaged offensive weapons sold on the dark web. The industry will observe the first genuine, widespread "agentic" intrusions, where an AI is deployed with a high-level goal (e.g., "Map the network and extract financial data from Company X") and autonomously navigates the environment, iterating its privilege escalation techniques based on real-time feedback. As a result, the speed of attacks will permanently outpace human response capabilities. With the Mean Time to Exfiltrate (MTTE) measured in seconds rather than minutes , defense will rely entirely on autonomous AI security agents countering offensive AI agents, removing the human analyst from the real-time detection and remediation loop entirely.5-Year Forecast (2031)Looking toward the next decade, the static malware paradigm will be largely obsolete. Malware will likely function as a lightweight, generic framework that queries sovereign, adversarial LLMs—hosted in bulletproof jurisdictions—to generate execution logic dynamically on the fly. Furthermore, geopolitical cyber warfare will shift focus. Nation-states will move away from simply stealing data or destroying networks, and instead focus on subtly poisoning the massive, unstructured datasets required to train future AI models. This will introduce long-term, virtually undetectable strategic biases, backdoors, or analytical blind spots directly into adversarial AI infrastructure.10. Final ConclusionsThe integration of Artificial Intelligence into the cyber threat ecosystem is absolute and irreversible. The industry has firmly transitioned out of the era where AI was merely an offline assistant utilized by scammers for crafting grammatically correct phishing emails. With the confirmed deployment of runtime AI malware by state actors (PROMPTSTEAL, PROMPTFLUX) and the weaponization of multimodal deepfakes in multi-million dollar corporate heists, the barrier between physical human identity and digital verification has effectively collapsed.However, the cybersecurity industry must rigorously delineate between verifiable, empirical threats and vendor-driven hysteria. The most acute risk facing organizations today is not a sentient AI independently hacking critical infrastructure. Rather, it is the industrialization of cybercrime by low-skilled actors leveraging LLMs to achieve unprecedented operational scale, combined with the extreme fragility of the enterprise AI software supply chain. As threat actors continue to ruthlessly optimize their workflows, defenders must fundamentally recalibrate their security postures. Static signature detection, legacy email gateways, and visual human verification must be abandoned in favor of deep behavioral analysis, strict zero-trust architectures for non-human identities, and AI-driven autonomous defense mechanisms capable of operating at machine speed.Strategic Appendices1. Enforce strict least-privilege policies, continuous credential rotation, and monitor agentic API calls for anomalous behavior.Mandate Cryptographic Out-of-Band Verification: Completely deprecate visual or audio confirmation for high-risk financial transactions or sensitive data access. Implement cryptographic, out-of-band verification (e.g., FIDO2 hardware keys or pre-registered, trusted phone callbacks) to neutralize the escalating threat of real-time deepfakes.Pin All AI Dependencies: Protect against the rising tide of AI supply chain attacks by pinning all open-source AI libraries and dependencies to exact versions using cryptographic hashes (e.g., lockfiles). Disable automatic, mutable version updates to prevent the automatic ingestion of trojanized code.Adopt Behavioral, Not Signature-Based, Detection: With malware families like PROMPTFLUX generating metamorphic code at runtime via LLM APIs , static indicators of compromise (IOCs) and traditional antivirus hashes are obsolete. Deploy AI-driven Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) that monitors for anomalous runtime behaviors and unusual outbound API connections to known LLM endpoints.Overhaul Anti-Phishing Training Paradigms: Traditional security awareness training that focuses on spotting spelling errors and poor grammar is entirely ineffective against AI-generated lures. Shift organizational focus to analyzing the context of the request, URL behavioral analysis, and implementing adaptive simulation training that utilizes safe, AI-generated polymorphic phishing variants to test employee resilience.
