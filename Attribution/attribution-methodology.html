About This Guide

This guide teaches attribution as an analytical discipline, not a lookup operation. The goal is not to memorize which nation-state uses which malware family — the goal is to understand how analysts build attribution cases, what makes evidence strong or weak, and how to communicate attribution conclusions with the epistemic honesty that separates credible intelligence from assertive noise.

Audience: CTI analysts, threat intelligence consumers, and security practitioners who need to build, defend, or challenge attribution claims.

What this guide covers:
- The five-level attribution spectrum (Unattributed → Individual Attribution) and when each level is achievable
- The four evidence types ranked by strength: IOC Overlap (weakest) → Infrastructure Reuse → TTP Consistency → Operator Mistakes (strongest)
- Why wrong attribution is worse than no attribution: IR misdirection, policy harm, false flag success, credibility loss
- The Attribution Confidence Model and how to apply it
- Cluster-level vs. incident-level attribution and when to use each
- False flag detection — how adversaries plant deceptive evidence and how to resist it
- A practical worked exercise: attributing APT29 / Cozy Bear / SVR
- How to articulate attribution statements with valid uncertainty bounds
- Common analyst mistakes and how to avoid them
- Interview-ready frameworks for communicating attribution in reports and to leadership
